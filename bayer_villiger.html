Baeyer–Villiger Oxidation

Definition

Baeyer–Villiger oxidation is an important organic reaction in which ketones (or aldehydes) are oxidized to esters (or carboxylic acids) using peroxy acids such as mCPBA. In this reaction, an oxygen atom is inserted adjacent to the carbonyl group.

General Reaction

Ketone + Peroxy acid → Ester + Carboxylic acid

Examples

1. Oxidation of Cyclohexanone

Cyclohexanone reacts with peracid to form ε-caprolactone.

2. Oxidation of Acetone

Acetone gives methyl acetate after oxidation.

3. Aldehyde Reaction

Aldehydes undergo oxidation to give carboxylic acids.

Mechanism

Going from a ketone to an ester is an oxidation process, but we mentioned that the mechanism of Baeyer-Villiger is different from what we saw in conventional reactions using PCC, KMnO4, etc. It is a rearrangement reaction where a tetrahedral peroxy intermediate undergoes a familiar 1,2-shift, thus forming the R-O alkoxy component of the ester. Applications

  • Used for the synthesis of esters and lactones.
  • Important in pharmaceutical and polymer industries.
  • Used in fragrance and fine chemical synthesis.
  • Helps in ring expansion reactions.

Limitations

  • Requires strong oxidizing agents (peracids).
  • Rearrangement depends on migration aptitude.
  • May produce side products in unsymmetrical ketones.
  • Sensitive functional groups may get oxidized.
