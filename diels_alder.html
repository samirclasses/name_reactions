Diels–Alder Reaction

Definition

The Diels–Alder reaction is a cycloaddition reaction between a conjugated diene and a dienophile to form a six-membered cyclic compound. It is a concerted reaction and forms two new σ-bonds simultaneously. It is named after Otto Diels and Kurt Alder, who discovered it in 1928.

General Reaction

Diene + Dienophile → Cyclohexene derivative

Example

1,3-butadiene + ethene → cyclohexene

Mechanism

The Diels–Alder reaction proceeds through a single-step concerted mechanism:
  • The π-electrons of the diene and dienophile reorganize simultaneously.
  • Two new σ-bonds are formed.
  • A new six-membered ring is produced.
  • No intermediates are formed.
  • The reaction is stereospecific (cis → cis product).

Key Features

  • Pericyclic reaction
  • Concerted mechanism
  • No catalyst required (but can be accelerated by heat or Lewis acids)
  • Forms cyclic compounds
  • Stereospecific reaction

Applications

  • Synthesis of cyclic compounds
